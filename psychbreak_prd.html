01 — Overview

What is PsychBreak?

PsychBreak is a mobile companion app for couples and close relationships. When a conversation escalates into anger or frustration, either person can tap the "Breakout" button. The app immediately launches a short, structured mini-game designed to lower cortisol, shift emotional states, and bring both people back to a calm and curious headspace — before the real conversation continues.

Core insight: Most arguments aren't about the argument. They are about two people in flooded emotional states who have lost access to their prefrontal cortex. Forcing a pause is not enough. The pause needs to do something. PsychBreak gives that pause a job.

The app is rooted in established psychology: emotional flooding theory (John Gottman), co-regulation, future-pacing, and active listening research. The games are not distractions — they are therapeutic tools wrapped in accessible, low-stakes formats.

02 — Problem Statement

Why This App Needs to Exist

When two people argue, the brain shifts into a defensive, threat-response mode. Physiologically, heart rate rises above 100 bpm, and rational thought becomes functionally impaired. Most couples in this state do one of three things: escalate further, go cold and silent, or walk away unresolved.

🔥

Escalation

Raised voices and defensiveness. The original issue is buried under the heat of the moment.

🧊

Stonewalling

One or both parties shut down. Nothing gets resolved. Resentment accumulates.

🚪

Avoidance

The conversation is abandoned. The same fight happens again a week later.

😶

Toxic repair

One person apologises just to end the fight, without genuine understanding or change.

There is no widely available, accessible tool that intervenes at this precise moment — the moment of peak emotional flooding — and actively helps two people de-escalate together, rather than apart.

04 — Core User Flow

The Breakout Journey

The entire core experience must be reachable in under 3 taps from the home screen. Speed matters: when someone is angry, they will not navigate a complex UI.

1

Trigger — The Breakout Button

Either party taps a large, prominent "Breakout Time" button on the home screen. No account required at this step. The button is the entire first screen. Big. Unmissable. One tap.

2

Invitation — Invite Your Partner

The app generates a unique session code or a shareable deep-link. The triggering user shares this with their partner via any messaging app. The partner taps the link and joins the same game session instantly. Both parties must be in the same session for the game to begin.

3

Breath — Mandatory Cool-Down Timer

Before the game starts, both users see a 60-second guided breathing animation. This is non-skippable. The science is clear: you cannot access rational thought until your heart rate drops below 100 bpm. This step is the most important one in the app, even if it feels simple.

4

Game — Play the Mini-Game

The app either selects a game randomly or lets users choose from the available options. Each game lasts 5–10 minutes. Both participants play simultaneously on their respective devices, coordinated in real-time via a lightweight backend.

5

Reflect — Post-Game Debrief Prompt

After the game, the app displays a single, gentle conversation prompt designed to ease the parties back into dialogue. Examples: "What surprised you about how your partner described something?" or "What future goal of theirs did you not know about?" There is no AI counselling here — just a question and space.

6

Return — Back to the Conversation

Both users optionally rate their current emotional state on a simple 1–5 scale (emoji-based, low cognitive load). If both are above 3/5, the app offers to guide them back to the original topic using a structured "I feel / I need / I ask" communication framework. If either is still below 3, the app suggests another game round or a longer break.

05 — The Games Library

Therapeutic Mini-Games

Each game in PsychBreak is chosen for a specific psychological function. They are not filler — each one targets a specific emotional or communicative mechanism that breaks down during conflict. Below are the four games for the v1 launch, including the two core games proposed in the brief.

Game 01 · Core

The Blind Architect

A communication and frustration-tolerance game used in corporate retreats and couples therapy alike. Deceptively simple; psychologically rich.

How to play

Both parties are placed back-to-back — represented on their respective phone screens. One person is given a randomly generated simple image (a house with a tree, a geometric shape, a simple animal outline). They must describe it to their partner using only shape names and spatial directions — no object names allowed. The partner draws it on a digital canvas on their phone. After 3 minutes, both screens are revealed side-by-side.

Why it works

This game makes miscommunication visible in a safe, non-threatening context. It teaches patience, active listening, and concretely demonstrates how differently two people interpret the exact same instructions — even when both are trying their very best. The shared laugh at the wildly different drawings is itself therapeutic.

Technical requirements:
  • Real-time WebSocket connection between two players
  • Simple canvas drawing tool (finger draw on mobile)
  • Image library of 50+ simple line-drawings (SVG preferred)
  • Side-by-side reveal animation at end of round
  • "No object names" word filter is optional in v1 — enforced by honour system
Game 02 · Core

Two Truths & a Dream

A future-pacing exercise disguised as the classic party game. It shifts focus from the past (where all fights live) to the future (where both people still share a timeline).

How to play

Each person is given 2 minutes to write down three things they want to happen in the next five years. Two must be genuine desires they have not yet shared with their partner. One is completely made up. They submit their three statements, then take turns reading them aloud (via text displayed on screen). The other person guesses which one is the invented dream. Points are optional — connection is the goal.

Why it works

This is applied future-pacing — a technique used in Neuro-Linguistic Programming and modern couples therapy. Arguments anchor people to the present moment or the past. By discussing future goals in a playful, low-stakes format, the game reminds both parties that they share a long-term timeline and opens up conversations about ambitions, fears, and desires that may have never surfaced otherwise.

Technical requirements:
  • Text input with 120-character limit per statement
  • Turn-based reveal logic (Player A reads, Player B guesses, then swap)
  • Timer for writing phase (2 minutes, optional)
  • Simple guess mechanic: tap the statement you think is the dream
  • Optional: save favourite responses to a "Dream Journal" in the app
Game 03 · Supplemental

The Gratitude Volley

A structured positive reframing exercise to counteract the negativity bias that dominates during conflict.

How to play

Players alternate typing one specific thing they are grateful for about the other person. The rule is specificity: "I love you" is not allowed. "I'm grateful that you make my coffee without being asked" is. They go back and forth for 8 rounds, building a real-time list on both screens simultaneously. At the end, the full list is shown to both players at once.

Why it works

Gottman's research shows that healthy relationships maintain a 5:1 ratio of positive to negative interactions. This game directly builds that ratio in the immediate aftermath of a negative exchange. Specificity is mandatory because generic praise is processed as empty by the brain, while specific praise activates genuine emotional response and memory.

Game 04 · Supplemental

The Emotion Translator

A non-verbal, low-pressure emotion mapping game for moments when words feel too hard.

How to play

Each player is shown a grid of 20 emotion words (e.g. "overlooked," "anxious," "proud," "exhausted"). They must privately select the 5 that best describe how they feel right now — not about the argument, but in general. Then both selections are revealed simultaneously. Players discuss: "Which of these surprised you? Which did you expect?"

Why it works

Emotional granularity — the ability to name specific emotions — is one of the strongest predictors of emotional regulation and relationship health. Many people in conflict say "I'm angry" when they are actually feeling scared, humiliated, or exhausted. This game builds the vocabulary for more precise self-expression and shows each partner that the emotional landscape of the other person is often more complex and sympathetic than it appears mid-argument.

07 — Design Principles

How This App Must Feel

🧘

Calm by default

No push notifications that feel urgent. No red badges. Soft animations. The app itself must model the emotional state it is trying to create.

⚡

Frictionless under stress

Maximum 3 taps to start a game session. Large touch targets. No modals requiring careful reading. People in conflict have zero patience for UI friction.

🎮

Playful, not clinical

No therapy language on surface-level UI. No "emotional regulation" or "attachment styles" in the main flow. Fun first, science underneath.

🤐

Privacy first

Sessions are ephemeral by default. Nothing is shared with third parties. Data stored on-device unless user explicitly opts into history sync.

🌈

Colour as emotion

The UI transitions from warm, energetic tones when the Breakout is triggered → cooler, softer blues and greens as the session progresses toward calm.

📵

No dark patterns

No addictive loops. No streaks. No gamification that creates obligation. The app's goal is to be used less over time as patterns improve.

Colour system suggestion: Begin session in warm amber/coral tones (active, urgent). Transition through breathing screen to a soft warm white. Land in cool sage/teal for game screens. This chromatic journey mirrors the emotional journey the app is facilitating.

08 — Technical Architecture

What Needs to Be Built

Platform

React Native (iOS + Android). Both partners need the app on their respective devices. A web companion (for tablets/desktop use as secondary device in a session) can be a v2 consideration.

Backend Requirements

ComponentPurposeTech Suggestion
Real-time session Sync game state between two players' devices WebSockets / Supabase Realtime / Firebase RTDB
Session codes Generate unique 6-digit join codes Simple server-side generation, Redis TTL
Drawing sync Relay canvas strokes in Blind Architect game WebSocket stroke events (delta, not full canvas)
Auth (optional v1) Persistent partner link, history storage Anonymous auth in v1, email/Google in v2
Session history Store mood check-ins and saved content Local SQLite in v1, Supabase Postgres in v2
Image library SVG drawings for Blind Architect Static CDN-hosted SVG files, bundled offline fallback

Offline Considerations

If both players are in the same room (the common case), a local P2P connection via Bluetooth or local WiFi should be the primary connection method. This removes latency, reduces costs, and works without internet. WebSocket cloud sync is the fallback for long-distance use cases.

Key constraint: The app must work reliably under poor network conditions. An argument is the worst possible time to encounter a loading spinner or connection error. Implement aggressive retry logic and a clear "waiting for partner" state that does not feel like a failure.
